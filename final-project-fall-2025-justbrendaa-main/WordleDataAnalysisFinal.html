Introduction

Wordle is a popular online word puzzle where players guess a five letter word within six attempts. Each guess provides feedback: letters in the correct position are highlighted in green, letters in the word but in the wrong position appear yellow, and letters not in the word are gray. Beyond being an engaging game, Wordle presents a rich dataset for analysis. Words have unique characteristics, such as vowel-consonant balance, repeated letters, rare letters, Scrabble scores, and frequency of usage.

This analysis explores these patterns across 1600+ Wordle words to uncover trends in word selection, letter placement, and other structural features. By examining the relationships between word properties, frequency, and temporal trends, this project aims to provide insights into the hidden patterns that shape the daily puzzle.

Questions to answer

  • Which letters appear more frequently in Wordle solutions?
  • Which letters appear more frequently in Wordle solutions by position?
  • Which letter pairs (bigrams) appear most often in Wordle solutions?
  • How does the bigram frequency change depending on where they appear in the word?
  • Do repeated letter Wordle answers follow seasonal or monthly patterns across different years?
  • Is there a relationship between a words frequency in English and its Scrabble point value?

Is there a relationship between a words frequency in English and its Scrabble point value?

Wordle words vary in both their frequency in the English language and their Scrabble score. One might expect that rarer words, which are less commonly used, would contain more unusual letters like Q, Z, or X and therefore have higher Scrabble scores. Conversely, more common words might use more frequent letters like E, A, or R and tend to have lower Scrabble values. By examining how Scrabble scores vary across words of different frequency categories, we can explore whether Wordle favors high-scoring words or if score and frequency are largely independent.



The violin plot shows the distribution of Scrabble scores for rare, uncommon, and common Wordle words. Interestingly, there is no strong correlation between a words frequency and its Scrabble score. Rare words do not consistently have higher scores, and common words do not always have lower scores.

Summary

This analysis of 1,600+ Wordle solutions uncovers both predictable patterns and surprising gaps in the games word selection:

  • Letter and positional patterns:
    • Words often start with common consonant clusters (ST, SH, BR) and end with frequent suffixes (ER, CH, GE).
    • Vowels tend to appear in middle positions, reflecting natural English structure.
  • Bigrams reveal structure:
    • Common letter pairs like ER, IN, and ST dominate specific positions, helping explain why certain guesses narrow possibilities faster.
  • Rare plurals:
    • Words ending in S or the bigram ES are extremely uncommon, confirming that Wordle generally avoids pluralizing four-letter words.
  • Repeated letters:
    • Words with repeated letters appear randomly across months and years, showing no seasonal or temporal patterns.
  • Scrabble scores vs.frequency:
    • High-scoring words are driven by rare letters (Z, Q, X), while common letters keep scores low.
    • Word frequency in English does not strongly predict Scrabble value.


Takeaway:

Wordle solutions may feel random, but they follow subtle English patterns. Recognizing these trends like common prefixes, suffixes, letter positions, and high value letters, can improve guessing strategies and give players a structured approach to solving puzzles.
